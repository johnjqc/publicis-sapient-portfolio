This section contains written responses describing real experiences from my professional career.

## Is there any situation that describes an unconventional route taken in your experience?
## If so, please share a paragraph or two about this.

During a certain period of my career, I wanted to grow professionally, but I felt stuck. I thought that simply improving my technical knowledge was the only way to achieve it.

When I changed my first job, I experienced a situation where my technical lead told me in a one-on-one meeting that he considered me a good engineer, but he expected more support from me in sharing ideas because I hardly participated in discussions. I explained that I was used to my previous job, where I only received task assignments and carried them out. That's when I realized that I was now in a company with a culture that expected me to help build the product. I started asking more questions, expressing my ideas, and challenging my colleagues' ideas, because I realized that good engineering is not only about writing code, but also about improving decisions through collaboration..

When I changed jobs again, I applied this behavior from the beginning. I quickly gained visibility and self-confidence. My behavior helped me grow professionally by building stronger relationships across the team and helped my team feel in a safe environment where we could all express our opinions and improving the team collaboration which led to better technical discussions and better engineering decisions.

Since then, I've believed that technical expertise opens opportunities, but collaboration, communication and influence are what allow you to create real impact.

## Do you recall a situation where you intensely and relentlessly focused to completa a task(can be professional work or hobby) that helped you grow?
## If so, please describe this experience in a paragraph or two.

The most typical situation is when I have to do something that is new to me; I always focus on understanding the problem so that I can study and research what is necessary to learn in order to solve said problem.

In one project I was the technical lead, but due to limited resources I also had to act as Product Owner, QA Lead and Agile Lead. Those responsibilities were outside my experience. Instead of seeing that as a limitation, my goal wasn't to do every role perfectly because I believed the project needed someone optimizing the overall outcome instead of protecting role boundaries, it was to make the project succeed. Throughout the project I remained focused on identifying, following and communicating progress, identifying and working to reduce or eliminate risks by continuously testing as critical features were added to the code, and removing blockers by talking to people or asking for help instead of complaining about the lack of resources. We delivered on time, which enabled the business team to achieve its goal of reducing notification delivery costs and that experience completely changed my perspective on leadership. 

I learned that achieving ambitious goals is often more about strategy, prioritization, and continuous learning than having unlimited resources.

## Describe an experience of joining new team, project or environment where there was significant amount of context, information or complexity to absorb in a relatively short period of time.
## How did you approach learning, building clarity, prioritizing what to focus on and gradually becoming effective?

My last experience in this situation was when I joined Mercado Libre. I entered an organization with many systems, various engineering practices and processes, and a lot of engineering information and context that I had to learn. My first approach was to establish relationships with the people who had been on the team longer, so they could guide me with questions or offer suggestions about information or processes. Since it's difficult to learn everything, I started by understanding our team's business domain and how we generated value within it. Then I tried to identify the components we owned, their architecture and responsibilities, and I sought to understand how they were monitored and the support processes the team followed.

To accelerate my learning, I used all the AI ​​tools I had access to, focused on the available documentation, studied the source code with AI, and maintained communication with expert engineers to resolve doubts. To generate value, I tried to understand the product priorities defined by the leader or manager and looked for improvement opportunities that I could propose. I began to participate in discussions and gradually gained ownership of the product.

I've learned that in complex environments, understanding the business and building relationships accelerate learning far more than trying to memorize every system.
